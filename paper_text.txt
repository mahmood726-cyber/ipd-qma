Title:  Unmasking Heterogeneous Treatment Effects in Clinical Trials: A "Location-Scale" Quantile Meta-Analysis Framework (IPD-QMA) ] Abstract Background R andomized controlled trial (RCT)  their  meta-analysis of RCTs  is the basis of  evidence-based medicine.  T raditional meta-analy sis  aggregate s  treatment effects  using Mean  Difference (MD)  as a summary measure . This approach  uses  the "location shift" assumptio n which is  that  the  treatment shifts the outcome distribution uniformly for all patients.  In more  complex medical domains such as psychiatry, oncology, and critical care  these  interventions  can create a  "location-scale shift," . This increases  outcome variance without changing the overall mean. Standard methods  may then  fail to detect efficacy in these scenarios . This  will  potentially  discard treatments that  can  benefit high-risk subgroups while harming  the  low-risk ones. Methods We introduce  a new method which is  the    Individual  Participant Data Quantile Meta-Analysis (IPD-QMA) . This Two-Stage framework u ses  Quantile Regression to estimate treatment effects across the severity distribution (τ = 0.1 to 0.9) within  studies . It then  pools these coefficients using a Random Effects model. We compared IPD-QMA against  the  Standard Meta-Analysis (Mean Difference) and the Log Variance Ratio ( lnVR ) using 10,000 Monte Carlo simulations. We  used this to  tes t  both Normal and Skewed (Exponential) distributions to assess robustness.  We also created  a "Slope Test" (Wald statistic) to formally test the hypothesis of effect homogeneity. Results In  "hidden utility" scenarios where a treatment  can increase  variance (scale × 3)  however  not  the mean .   In these  Standard Meta-Analysis demonstrated a statistical power of only 5.3% (Normal distribution) to 14.0% (Skewed distribution) .   This stops if from  effectively failing to detect any therapeutic signal. IPD-QMA  in comparison  demonstrated  100% power  to detect significant benefits at the 90th percentile.  T he Log Variance Ratio ( lnVR ) detected heterogeneity with high  power,  but  it lacked directionality . It was  unable to  truly  distinguish benefit from harm. IPD-QMA  in contrast gave a  detailed "Quantile Profile" . This  reveal ed  that the treatment was harmful for mild cases but highly effective for severe cases. Conclusion IPD-QMA  can be a  necessary diagnostic tool for precision medicine. SO  while    lnVR  acts as a screening tool for heterogeneity  in contrast  IPD-QMA characterizes the shape of the effect . This can  preven t  the loss of potentially life-saving interventions for high-risk populations. We  also provide an  open -source software implementation to  help  the adoption of this method. Introduction The Limits of the Average Systematic reviews and meta-analyses  form the  highest level of evidence in clinical medicine [1]. These are designed to  take  data from multiple trials to estimate a "true" treatment effect with greater precision than any single study.  Most  meta-analyses rely on summary statistics—specifically measures of central tendency such as the Mean Difference (MD), Standardized Mean Difference (SMD), or Odds Ratio (OR)—to aggregate results [2]. I n this emphasis on  the  mean  is the statistical assumption of a "location shift." This  is the assumption that  the  biological  action of a treatment  will  shift the outcome distribution of the treated population uniformly relative to the control group.  We assume the  "average patient" is a valid proxy for the entire population.  C linical reality is  rarely uniform . Patients  even  within a clinical trial differ  significantly in  genetic predisposition, disease severity, comorbidities, and baseline risk. These factors  then  interact with the treatment . This leads  to  Heterogeneity of Treatment Effects (HTE)  [3]. The Location-Scale Problem A  difficult  form of heterogeneity is the "location-scale shift." In this an intervention does not merely shift the mean (location)  but also changes  the spread (scale) of the outcome distribution [4]. Consider a  novel  vasodilator administered for sepsis. For patient s  in mild shock (low risk), the drug  may  cause dangerous hypotension . This can  lead to worse outcomes (a negative effect at the lower tail).  Yet for  a patient in severe septic shock (high risk), the same drug might restore perfusion . This could in turn  prevent organ failure (a positive effect at the upper tail).  So, the trial   enrols  a mix of mild and severe patients . In that case  these  opposing effects may algebraically cancel each other out. The resulting Mean Difference would be near zero. A standard meta-analysis of such trials  could  conclude that the treatment is "ineffective" (p &gt; 0.05). This  while  statistically accurate regarding the  average , is clinically  wrong . It  causes the  the abandonment of a therapy that is  life-saving  for the sickest patients . This is  only   because  it is toxic to the healthiest [5]. Limitations of Current Solutions The current toolkit for handling heterogeneity  at this moment includes  subgroup analysis and meta-regression. While useful, these methods have  known and  significant limitations: Low Power:  Subgroup analyses often lack statistical power to detect differences . This is because  they split the sample size [6]. Ecological Fallacy:  Meta-regression relies on study-level aggregate data (e.g., "percentage of patients with severe disease" ). To   correlate   study -level averages with study-level effects can lead to the ecological fallacy . This  is   where  relationships observed across groups do not  actually  hold for individuals [7]. Arbitrary Cutoffs:  Defining subgroups (e.g., "Age &gt; 65" or "Sepsis Score &gt; 10") requires arbitrary dichotomization . This in turn  results in a loss of information. Recently, the analysis of variance itself has  started to see more new work . The Log Variance Ratio ( lnVR )  was  proposed as a metric to compare the variability of the treatment group versus the control group [8].  A  significant  lnVR  is  a  "red flag" that indicates heterogeneity  exists .   I t is  however  a  non-specific screening tool. It cannot tell  a  clinician  who  is affected. A treatment group might have higher variance because the drug helps everyone variably . That is  because  it helps some and hurts others. The Proposal: IPD-QMA To address we  have created  a framework based on  Quantile Regression  [9]. Unlike ordinary least squares regression, which estimates the conditional mean, quantile regression  instead  estimates the conditional median (0.50) or any other quantile (e.g., 0.10, 0.90) of the outcome distribution. Through  applying this to meta-analysis, we construct a "Quantile Profile" of the treatment effect.  So rather than  asking, "Does the treatment work?" .  Instead  we can ask, "For which slice of the severity distribution does the treatment work?" In this paper  we present   Individual Participant Data Quantile Meta-Analysis (IPD-QMA) . We  lay out  a   Two -Stage computational framework for this  method . We then  validate it through extensive Monte Carlo simulations involving both Normal and skewed distribution s. This  demonstrate s  its  true  ability  to "unmask" treatment effects that standard methods miss. "One-Stage" models ( analysing  all patients in a single hierarchical model)  can be  attractive .  Two-Stage models are often  more used  in practice due to their computational efficiency  and  convergence stability . They are also  ability to easily handle summary steps using standard meta-analytic software. Visualization and P-value Distribution We  looked at  the discriminatory power of each model by plotting the resulting p-values.  Though  a true null effect typically produces a Uniform Distribution (where p is distributed evenly between 0 and 1), an effective model should demonstrate a sharp "pile-up" near zero. With   the  extreme sensitivity of the IPD-QMA approach in this scenario,  the majority of  its p-values  were  concentrated in the p &lt; 0.001 bin. Discussion The Fallacy of the Average The central finding of this study is that standard meta-analysis i s   mostly  blind to location-scale shifts. In  th ese  simulations, a treatment that was lifesaving for the sickest 10% of patients was deemed "ineffective" by standard methods because it was  cancelled  out by harm to the healthiest 10%.  T his corresponds to a "failed" Phase 3 trial  in a clinical context .  The   trial did not fail because the drug was inert .   It  failed because the statistical summary (the mean) was insufficient to  show the reality.  Screening vs. Diagnosis: The  lnVR  Comparison T his paper compar es  the Log Variance Ratio ( lnVR ) and IPD-QMA. Our results show that  lnVR  has excellent power (100%) to reject the null hypothesis.  Then why  implement the more complex IPD-QMA if  lnVR  works? We  would argue that   lnVR  is a screening tool , whereas  IPD-QMA is a diagnostic tool . A significant  lnVR   says to a  researcher:  "The treatment group outcome is more variable than the control."  This  should be  an alarm bell.  This might  mean the treatment helps the sick and hurts the healthy (as in our simulation).  It could  also mean the treatment is simply chaotic . This might be  adding random noise to everyone.  It  could  also  mean the treatment pushes everyone toward the extremes. IPD-QMA deconvolutes th e  signal. It  gives  the  directionality   needed  for clinical decision-making. It  can  tell the clinician:  "This treatment increases variance specifically by improving outcomes for severe patients  but  worsen s  them for mild patients." W e  suggest  a hierarchical workflow . R esearchers can calculate  lnVR  as a quick check for heterogeneity. If  lnVR  is  found to be  significant, IPD-QMA should be performed to characterize the shape of that heterogeneity. Robustness and Stability Our  simulation on Skewed (Exponential) data  did  highlight a subtle but  crucial  flaw in standard meta-analysis. The Mean Difference method  resulted in a  14 % rejection rate when the null was true (Mean Diff = 0). This  tells us  that in the presence of heavy tails and unequal variances, the parametric assumptions of standard meta-analysis break down .    This  lead s  to unpredictable false positives. Quantile Regression  however  is semi-parametric. It does not assume  a  normality of the error terms. Our results  are that  IPD-QMA maintains high power and stability even when data is highly skewed . This makes it  particularly suitable for healthcare cost data, length of stay, or biomarker concentrations. Limitations and Barriers The primary limitation of IPD-QMA is  a  requirement for Individual Participant Data.  S tandard meta-analysis   which can be conducted using summary tables (Means and SDs) from PDF s.  IPD-QMA requires the raw row-level data. O btaining IPD  used to be  a prohibitive barrier. However, the landscape of clinical research is  changing   toward  transparency. Initiatives such as the  Yale Open Data Access (YODA)  project,  ClinicalStudyDataRequest.com , and the  COMET  initiative are  made  IPD increasingly accessible [9].  J ournals now  often  mandate data sharing statements.  Another  limitation is computational cost.  Our  Bootstrapping  of  20  studies with 200 iterations each requires significantly more processing power than a simple weighted average.  T he entire analysis of a single meta-analysis took less than 1 second on a standard laptop  for this paper . With modern computing  this is not an issue. Future Directions Future work  will hopefully  explore  One-Stage IPD-QMA models  using hierarchical Bayesian frameworks. A One-Stage model  may  theoretically handle smaller sample sizes more effectively . This is by  borrowing strength across studies for the estimation of tail quantiles.  E xpanding this framework to handle time-to-event data (Quantile Survival Analysis)  may  be valuable for oncology trials. Conclusion Precision medicine requires  good  statistics. The "average treatment effect"  we feel  is a blunt instrument that  can  obscure the true value of an intervention. We present  IPD-QMA , a robust framework for detecting and characterizing heterogeneous treatment effects. Our simulations demonstrate that IPD-QMA can  work on  trials that fail standard analysis due to location-scale shifts.  Through showing  the hidden utility in the tails of the distribution method enables a more nuanced understanding of efficacy .
